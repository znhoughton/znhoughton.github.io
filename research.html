Research

Language is not a fixed system that speakers look up and apply — it is a dynamic cognitive resource, continuously reshaped by every encounter we have with it. At the heart of my work is a question about the architecture of that resource: how do humans and machines trade off between storage and computation?

On the storage side, linguistic knowledge can be represented holistically — whole phrases, collocations, and constructions stored as units in memory, their form and meaning shaped directly by experience. Frequent patterns become entrenched. On the computation side, we assemble meaning from parts: abstract rules and schemas that apply broadly, regardless of specific experience. Most linguistic behavior doesn't fall cleanly into either camp.

What I study is the balance between them — how much exposure is needed to shift a pattern from computed to stored, how that shift manifests cognitively and behaviorally, and whether the computational systems we build for language mirror or diverge from the human solution. I approach these questions using neural networks, Bayesian models, and error-driven learning frameworks, alongside corpus analysis and experimental psycholinguistic methods including eye-tracking.

Mental Representations

Meaning isn't stored in a single place — it's distributed.

There is no single neuron or brain region that "holds" the word dog. Meaning emerges from patterns of activation distributed across millions of interconnected neurons, shaped by every context in which that word has appeared. Click words from the two groups below — notice that words within the same category activate overlapping but distinct patterns, while words from different categories share only a small core.

Learning

Prediction error drives representational change.

A core claim of usage-based linguistics is that linguistic knowledge is learned — not innate — and that the mechanism is error-driven: representations are updated proportionally to how wrong a prediction was. The Rescorla-Wagner model formalizes this: ΔV = αβ(λ − ΣV), where α is cue salience, β is learning rate, λ is the maximum association, and ΣV is the current total predictive strength. This simple rule captures counterintuitive effects like blocking, extinction, and overexpectation.

Computational Modeling

How much of language is memorized, and how much is assembled?

One test case for the storage-computation tradeoff is binomial expressions — phrases like bread and butter or black and white, where word order is conventionalized. A purely compositional system treats both orderings as equivalent, since averaging word vectors is commutative. A holistic system treats the canonical form as a distinct stored unit, making the two orderings distinguishable.

The visualization below uses GloVe word embeddings (reduced to 2D via PCA) to illustrate this. Each binomial is shown as a pair of dots — the canonical form (●) and its reversal (○). Drag the slider toward Holistic: the two orderings separate, with high-frequency binomials (larger, brighter) diverging more than low-frequency ones.

Noisy-Channel Comprehension

If high-frequency binomials are stored holistically, they should have a strong prior over their canonical order. This interacts with comprehension through a noisy-channel model (Levy 2008; Morgan & Levy 2016): listeners assume the input string may have been corrupted by noise (word transpositions, etc.), and infer the most probable intended form via Bayesian inference.

Formally: P(intended | heard) ∝ P(heard | intended) × P(intended). The prior P(intended) is shaped by frequency — high-frequency binomials have a strong prior for their canonical order. The likelihood P(heard | intended) is the noise model. Select a binomial, choose which form you "heard," and adjust the noise level.

Statistics

Quantifying uncertainty, updating beliefs from data.

Most of my work uses Bayesian regression as its primary analysis tool — not just for flexibility, but because it makes assumptions explicit and quantifies uncertainty rather than collapsing it to a p-value. The demo below shows the underlying mechanics. Imagine you're playing Werewolf: 3 of 10 players are secretly werewolves, so your prior probability that any given player is a werewolf is about 30%. Each round you observe that player's behavior — suspicious or innocent — and update. Watch the distribution narrow as evidence accumulates.
