Research

Language is not a fixed system that speakers look up and apply — it is a dynamic cognitive resource, continuously reshaped by every encounter we have with it. At the heart of my work is a question about the architecture of that resource: how do humans and machines trade off between storage and computation?

On the storage side, linguistic knowledge can be represented holistically — whole phrases, collocations, and constructions stored as units in memory, their form and meaning shaped directly by experience. Frequent patterns become entrenched. On the computation side, we assemble meaning from parts: abstract rules and schemas that apply broadly, regardless of specific experience. Most linguistic behavior doesn't fall cleanly into either camp.

What I study is the balance between them — how much exposure is needed to shift a pattern from computed to stored, how that shift manifests cognitively and behaviorally, and whether the computational systems we build for language mirror or diverge from the human solution. I approach these questions using neural networks, Bayesian models, and error-driven learning frameworks, alongside corpus analysis and experimental psycholinguistic methods including eye-tracking.

Mental Representations

Meaning isn't stored in a single place — it's distributed.

When you know a word, there is no single neuron or region that "holds" it. Instead, meaning emerges from patterns of activation distributed across millions of interconnected neurons, shaped by every context in which that word has appeared. Learning

Prediction error drives representational change.

A core claim of usage-based linguistics is that linguistic knowledge is learned — not innate — and that the mechanism of learning is error-driven: representations are updated proportionally to how wrong a prediction was. The Rescorla-Wagner model formalizes this: on each trial, the associative weight between a cue and an outcome updates as ΔV = αβ(λ − ΣV), where α is cue salience, β is learning rate, λ is the maximum possible association, and ΣV is the current total associative strength. The model captures why some associations are learned and others aren't — including the surprising blocking effect.

Computational Modeling

How much of language is memorized, and how much is assembled?

One test case for the storage-computation tradeoff is binomial expressions — phrases like bread and butter or black and white, where the order of the two words is conventionalized. High-frequency binomials may be stored holistically, making them resistant to reversal; low-frequency binomials may be assembled compositionally each time, making order less entrenched.

The visualization below uses GloVe word embeddings reduced to 2D via PCA. The slider moves between two theoretical positions: compositional (meaning derived from the average of the two words' vectors) and holistic (high-frequency binomials pushed outward, reflecting stronger entrenchment as single units). Statistics

Quantifying uncertainty, updating beliefs from data.

Much of my work uses Bayesian regression as a primary analysis tool — not just for its flexibility, but because it makes assumptions explicit and quantifies uncertainty rather than papering over it. The Bayesian framework also maps naturally onto cognitive models of learning: both describe how beliefs update in response to evidence. The demo below shows this updating process directly. Each observation shifts the posterior distribution over an unknown rate — start with no assumptions and watch the evidence accumulate.
