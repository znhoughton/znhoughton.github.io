// things that changed how I think

📖

Phonology and Language Use

Bybee · 2001

The book that showed me that the way we use language shapes the way it's represented. Our linguistic knowledge isn't a fixed system of rules — it's something that gets shaped by usage, continuously, over time.

📖

Abstract knowledge versus direct experience in processing of binomial expressions

Morgan & Levy · 2016

Made the case that humans track both abstract preferences and item-specific experience when processing multi-word expressions — not one or the other. Directly shaped my dissertation.

📖

Computational Explorations in Cognitive Neuroscience

O'Reilly & Munakata · 2000

The book that made neural networks feel like a theory of mind rather than a machine learning technique. Dense representations, bidirectional processing, error-driven learning — all grounded in actual neuroscience.

📖

The Neuroscience of Language

Pulvermüller · 2002

Grounded language in sensorimotor circuitry. Word meanings aren't abstract symbols floating in a void — they're patterns of neural activation tied to embodied experience. Changed how I think about what a mental representation actually is.

📖

Changing Minds Changing Tools

Kapatsinski · 2018

Connected learning theory, language acquisition, and language change into a single coherent framework. The book that made me realize these are all the same problem viewed from different timescales.

📄

The effect of usage on degrees of constituency: the reduction of don't in English

Bybee & Scheibman · Linguistics · 1999

Showed that "I don't know" is phonetically reduced as a holistic unit — evidence that frequently used phrases get stored whole, not assembled from parts each time. A clean, elegant argument for usage-based storage, and the foundation of many of the research questions my dissertation builds upon.

// fun linguistic fact

There is no single place in your brain where the word "dog" lives. Linguistic knowledge is stored as dense, distributed representations — patterns of activation spread across millions of interconnected neurons, shaped by every encounter you've ever had with that word. The meaning, the sound, the contexts it appeared in, how often you heard it, who said it: all of it is encoded not in any one location, but in the geometry of a high-dimensional space. That's what makes language so robust, so flexible, and so resistant to clean formal description. The grammar isn't stored anywhere. It emerges.
